Designing for accessibility

A Home Office digital project led by two Interaction Design Interns

Brief

Create a website for accessibility guidance, based on the popular ‘Designing for Accessibility’ posters.

View the work in progress

From physical to digital

The original posters began as a project by Karwai Pun, to give quick guidance on designing for people with dyslexia, autism, low vision, physical disabilities or hearing impairments, and people who use screenreaders. Designers around the world have praised the posters and collaborated on refining the content or translating them into different languages.

It was decided that an html version would be useful and also allow the guidance to be more detailed, so Megan Rodger and I were tasked with designing and building it.

As a first version, we’ve created a site that closely mirrors the original posters, which you can view here. After going straight into coding the desktop version, we stepped back, designed for mobile first and worked upwards.

Designing for new content

But this project is mainly a chance to act on feedback and expand on the guidance in the posters. What makes a sentence or colour scheme ‘simple’ and why is that preferable? What is ‘good’ contrast?

So for the second iteration we are focusing on designing for this new content, with the help of content designers and access needs experts around the Home Office.

Opening up for feedback

From presenting our work around government, we’ve had some great ideas for future changes. One suggestion is that the site could be shown to stakeholders when making the case for accessibility, so detailed case studies might be added to encourage empathy with people’s struggles.

We are completely open to feedback or collaboration with people in or outside government, so please feel free to get in touch.
