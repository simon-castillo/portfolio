Mapping research participants

Helping the Home Office to test its products with a broad set of users,
so our services work for everyone that needs them.

Services that work for all users

The digital inclusion (DI) scale is a way to measure someone’s skills, access and attitude to digital, going from people who ‘never have and never will’ use technology to experts with advanced skills. By testing our products with users across the scale, we can improve them and be more confident that everyone will be able to use Home Office services.

The goal of this project is to help researchers categorise the people they’ve tested with, and prove they are testing with a wide enough range of people.

Uncovering the real problem

But as testing went on we questioned the fundamental need for this tool, so we completed a short discovery, interviewing potential stakeholders and users. It became clear that the high-level, dashboard view of all Home Office projects was the primary need. User researchers themselves do not need a tool, although they would provide the data.

Rapid prototyping to test solutions

Now, in alpha, we are testing ways to capture high quality data while minimising the impact on user researchers.

I have prototyped simple, efficient ways to record participants' categories, and access guidance on digital inclusion only when its needed. And to develop a shared understanding of the scale amongst user researchers, I designed training material for upcoming face to face workshops.

As we approach the end of alpha, we are converging on a single end-to-end concept and assessing the requirements for beta.
